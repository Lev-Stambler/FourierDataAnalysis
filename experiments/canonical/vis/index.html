Inside the canonical q-ary Dataset-GL experiment

What the Fourier transform is actually applied to, how a 248,077-token alphabet becomes 133 bits per token, what the GL tree searches, and where every KL number comes from. A companion to experiments/canonical/qary_lsh_dataset_gl.py.

0The whole experiment in one picture

The experiment asks one question: how much of a language model's next-token distribution is captured by a tiny, interpretable Fourier series — and does the answer depend on how you encode tokens as bits? The teacher is Qwen3.5-0.8B. The student is a linear softmax model over ±1 parity features of the encoded context — no attention, no MLP, just certified Walsh characters. Everything else in the pipeline exists to make that comparison exact and honest.

The headline: 1,220 exactly-certified characters (220 single bits + 1,000 bit pairs) over sign-LSH codes recover a third of the teacher's non-unigram information on untouched test fibers, and the same recipe on random codes is 0.097 nats worse. The encoding — not extra capacity — is what the gap measures.

pipeline: make_data → build_lsh_codes / control_codes → spectrum_deg1/spectrum_deg2 → fit_sparse2 → model_sparse2_lsh.npz (2 MB, 625k params). Numbers: README findings 0–7.

1Fibers from the dataset, fills from the model

A fiber is an anchor in real text: a FineWeb span of 128 tokens whose first 67 tokens are kept as a ■ real prefix. The last K = 3 prefix tokens are the ■ fixed string s, and fibers are deduplicated on s — that is what makes f(window) = teacher(next | prefix(s), window) a well-defined function of the window instead of a relation.

The remaining 61 positions are ■ filled by the teacher itself, autoregressively, R = 8 independent times per fiber. This is the trick that makes Fourier analysis on a dataset possible at all: the model is its own natural-language sampler, so two fills of one fiber are two draws from P(g | s) — exactly the paired samples the CSAMP estimator of the paper needs. Each completed window is labeled with the teacher's terminal next-token distribution, and duplicate windows are collapsed into rows carrying weighted target sums A and counts n.

One fiber, schematically. The gray prefix comes from FineWeb (real tokens, drawn once); the amber tokens are the dedup key s; the blue span is filled by Qwen 8 times. The blue tokens shown are a real 61-token window from prompts.js; the fills fan out into 8 windows per fiber, each ending in a 512-slot teacher distribution.
draw_fibers (dedup on s, qary_lsh_dataset_gl.py:129), _fill_and_label (:748), soft_collapse (:157). M=8000 fibers × 8 fills = 512k windows for the headline numbers.

2What exactly the Fourier transform is applied to

Not tokens, and not the whole context — bits of the filled span. Every one of the 61 filled tokens is replaced by its B = 133-bit code (§4), newest token first, giving the boolean domain

$$ f:\ \{0,1\}^{n}\to\mathbb{R}^{512},\qquad n \;=\; 61\times 133 \;=\; 8113 .$$

The value f(x) is vector-valued: the teacher's terminal distribution over 512 slots. And crucially the search target is the centered residual \( F(x)=f(x)-\mu \) — subtracting the unigram \( \mu \) kills the “bias artifact” characters whose coefficient is large but parallel to the unigram and carries zero conditional information (in the code, A_search = A − n·μ). The Fourier basis is the Walsh characters, one per bit-mask \( S \):

$$ \chi_S(x) = (-1)^{\langle S,\,x\rangle},\qquad \hat f(S) = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{x}\chi_S(x)\,A_x \;\in\;\mathbb{R}^{512},\qquad \text{certified when } \lVert \hat f(S)\rVert_2 \ge \tau/2 .$$

A character's degree (in tokens) is how many token blocks its mask touches. A degree-1 character is a single LSH bit of a single context token — “is the newest token on the positive side of hyperplane 17?” A degree-2 character is the parity (agreement) of two such bits. Only the filled span is searchable: a character on dataset-fixed prefix bits is a constant sign inside each fiber, and constants square away in every bucket weight — the dataset cannot certify them. “Search more context” therefore literally means “make the model fill more tokens.”

The real domain: 61 token blocks × 133 LSH bits (one row per token, newest at top) for a real window. Dark cells are 1-bits. Overlaid: ● the model's certified deg-1 anchor bits on that token, and ▲ one real certified pair — a degree-2 character connecting a newest-token bit to a bit 3 tokens back.

3What the KL divergence is measured on

The 248k-token vocabulary is projected to a 512-slot alphabet: the 511 tokens with the highest mean terminal mass across sample fibers, plus an OTHER slot that absorbs the whole tail. Every window's label is the teacher's softmax squashed onto these slots. That is the distribution P below — the thing the student has to match.

The real slot alphabet, from model_sparse2_lsh.npz: mean terminal mass μ per slot (log scale, top 40 shown). Half of all terminal mass lands in OTHER — the teacher is often confident about a token that is not among the top 511. The remaining slots are the familiar suspects: ␣the, ,, ., ␣and…

The student never sees tokens. It sees \( K \) parity features \( \varphi(x)\in\{\pm1\}^K \) — the certified characters — and fits one weight per (character, slot):

$$ \mathrm{logits}(x) = \varphi(x)\,W + b,\qquad \mathrm{KL} \;=\; \frac{\sum_r n_r \sum_{v} P_{rv}\,\log\!\big(P_{rv}/Q_{rv}\big)}{\sum_r n_r}, $$

with \( Q = \mathrm{softmax}(\mathrm{logits}) \), rows weighted by their collapse counts \( n_r \). Three honesty devices matter as much as the formula: the bias \( b \) is initialized at the log-unigram, so an empty feature set scores exactly the unigram floor (1.6304) and any KL below it is real conditional signal; the train/val/test split is fiber-disjoint, so memorizing a fiber's prefix cannot leak; and the fit keeps the best-validation iterate, so a model never ends worse than its initialization — which is why “val_kl == unigram_kl” reads as found nothing, a bug signal, not a result.

KL playground

KL is asymmetric and locally quadratic — drag the student's bars and watch which slots bill you. The “unigram student” preset is the real μ over the top 12 slots: this is the floor every model in the arc is measured against.

4Making 248,077 tokens binary: sign-LSH

The GL machinery is boolean, but the alphabet is q-ary with q ≈ 218. The bridge is a locality-sensitive binary code: take the teacher's own input embeddings \( e_i \in \mathbb R^{d} \), mean-center them, and slice the cloud with 128 random Gaussian hyperplanes:

$$ \mathrm{code}_j(i) \;=\; \mathbb 1\big[\langle e_i-\bar e,\; g_j\rangle > 0\big], \qquad \Pr_j\big[\mathrm{code}_j(i)\ne\mathrm{code}_j(i')\big] \;=\; \frac{\theta(e_i,e_{i'})}{\pi}. $$

Tokens whose embeddings point the same way share most bits (5 tie-break bits separate byte-identical embedding rows, hence B = 133). Each bit is therefore a semantic dichotomy shared by the entire vocabulary — one side of one hyperplane. That is why a single degree-1 character can carry signal: “newest token is on the animal-ish side of hyperplane 17” generalizes across every token on that side. The control code (ctrl) has the same number of bits and the same uniqueness, but each bit is a random half of the vocabulary — identical capacity, zero geometry. The entire encoding effect in this experiment is the gap between those two columns.

5Finding heavy characters I: the exact spectrum

Below degree ~3, don't search — enumerate. All 8,113 degree-1 coefficients are one GEMM: \( \widehat F = \frac{1}{m} S^\top A_c \) with \( S \) the ±1 sign matrix. Anchored degree-2 is one more GEMM per certified anchor (multiply \( S \) elementwise by the anchor column, transform again). This is exact — no τ, no frontier, no sampling — and it is what produced the shipped model: 220 anchors and the top 1,000 pairs, all with \( \lVert\hat f\rVert_2 \ge 0.01 \).

All 220 certified degree-1 characters of the real model. x = which token (0 = newest), y = which LSH bit, area = fitted weight norm ‖W-row‖. The marginal histogram is the story: 128 / 53 / 21 / 16 / 1 / 1 — conditional signal concentrates hard at the prediction site.
The 1,000 certified degree-2 pairs. Every pair has one endpoint in the newest token block; the other endpoint reaches back — 794 stay within the newest token (bit×bit interactions), the tail hooks up to 57 tokens deep. Arc height ∝ weight norm.

The degree-2 spectrum is where the encodings truly separate: LSH has 485,000 certified pairs (27% of those checked) vs ctrl's 19,800 (1.4%) — a 25× low-degree-concentration gap. Both encodings converge as degree grows; LSH's edge is needing less degree for the same function.

6Finding heavy characters II: the GL tree and the paired estimator

Enumeration dies combinatorially at higher degree; the Goldreich–Levin tree survives by searching prefixes of masks. At level \( k \) the first \( k \) bits of the mask are decided and the rest are marginalized by grouping rows on their un-split suffix values \( z \). The bucket weight of a prefix \( S \) is the exact Parseval mass of all its completions:

$$ W_k(S) \;=\; \frac{2^{\,n-k}}{m^2}\sum_{t}\sum_{z} \Big(\sum_{x\in z}\chi_S(x)\,A[x,t]\Big)^{\!2}, \qquad \text{keep the child iff } W_k(S)\ \ge\ \tau^2/4 , $$

with the final level an exact leaf test. τ is the only statistical dial; max_width exists purely as a blow-up abort. The subtlety that cost several runs: this group-by square keeps the diagonal \( \sum_x\lVert f(x)\rVert^2 \), an S-independent mass that ties every bucket wherever suffix groups are near-singletons. The paper's estimator removes it by pairing: cells are (fiber × un-split value), only off-diagonal within-cell products count, and singleton cells cancel exactly —

$$ \psi_k(a) \;=\; \frac{Q_k(a) - \sum_x\lVert f(x)\rVert^2}{n_{\text{pairs}}}, \qquad Q_k(a) \;=\; \sum_{\text{cells}}\Big\lVert\sum_{x\in\text{cell}} f(x)\,\chi_a(x)\Big\rVert^2 . $$

Walk the tree yourself

A 10-bit toy with planted characters. Step through the levels and watch the frontier: at early levels (suffix groups all singletons) every child has identical weight — the amber “tie band” — and a width-capped frontier tie-breaks arbitrarily. That is frontier burial, the failure that produced the bogus “noise ceiling 0.006” before enumeration found 217 real characters. Then flip the estimator toggle with peaked fibers on, and watch the group-by square lift every bucket while paired ψ stays pinned to the truth.

The failure gallery

Each of these cost a run; each is now test-pinned. They are worth knowing because every one looks like a scientific result (“no signal”) and is actually an estimator artifact.

Frontier burial In the singleton-suffix region all 2k prefixes provably tie, the bounded frontier tie-breaks arbitrarily, and each deg-1 leaf gets exactly one chance to be carried. Read as “noise ceiling 0.006, 1/√m”. Fix: enumerate deg-1/2 exactly; tree only for degree ≥ 3.
Tie-saturation / point-mass inflation Deep 399-bit descent: peaked fibers make χS(i)χS(j) = 1 for all S, a per-fiber constant that floods the width-512 frontier with ψ ≈ 0.0235 high-degree masks. Fit floors at exactly unigram (1.8287). Fix: floor-at-unigram is a bug signal; clean subtraction removes the same-token block.
Uncentered bias characters Raw targets admit characters with huge ‖f̂‖ but f̂ ∥ μ — zero conditional content (the idbits artifact). Fix: search the centered residual A − n·μ; let the intercept carry the unigram.
Fiber memorization Full-context deg-1 students read prefix bits as fiber IDs and memorize; fiber-disjoint validation floors them at unigram. Fix: filled-span features only, or staged val-gated blocks (fit_staged: accepts exactly the last 3–4 tokens, KL 1.1232).

7The collision-free fix: forked-cache pairs

The flat-table paired estimator needs two fills that collide on every un-split coordinate — rare exactly where the search is deep. The oracle removes luck from the construction: forward each fiber's prefix (plus older fills, a shared stub) once, fork the transformer cache G ways, and resample only the split token onward. Every one of the G(G−1) within-fiber ordered pairs is now valid by construction:

$$ \psi(b) \;=\; \frac{1}{n_{\text{pairs}}}\sum_{z} \Big( \big\lVert \textstyle\sum_{i\in z}\chi_b(i)\,F_i \big\rVert^2 \;-\; \textstyle\sum_{i\in z}\lVert F_i\rVert^2 \Big), $$

unbiased for the conditional weight \( \mathbb E_z\lvert\mathbb E[F\chi_b\,|\,z]\rvert^2 \). The clean=True variant additionally subtracts the same-token block — pairs whose split token is identical satisfy χb(i)χb(j)=1 for every b, a per-fiber floor that cannot change rankings but floods any fixed certification bar.

One fiber: the shared stub is forwarded once (Cache.reorder_cache forks KV and Qwen3.5's linear-attention conv/recurrent state), then G = 16 branches resample the split token. A fail-loud self-check asserts branch 0 matches a fresh forward (max slot err ~6e-3).
The paper-native estimator reproduces both the recovery and the encoding gap where the flat tree floored: held-out KL after fitting the certified split-token bits, fill-3 frame, M1500 G16.
Sweep of the split position: deg-1 gain vs unigram at the newest / middle / oldest filled token (LSH −0.368 / −0.059 / ≈0 nats). Signal decays with distance from the prediction; LSH is ~2× ctrl wherever there is signal, and the oracle correctly reports ≈0 where there is none. Clean certification: LSH certifies all 133 bits of the newest token; ctrl only ~67 (its “signal” was mostly the point-mass floor).

9The “ceiling” at degree 2 — and what it actually was

The arc's first conclusion (README finding 0b): the ladder terminates at degree 2 — 1,000 certified triples exist (top norm 0.0604, heavier than any pair!) yet val-gated incremental fitting pins their weights at exactly zero, and a flat joint refit degrades test KL 1.103 → 1.192. Redundant-but-heavy is real: triples are products of already-captured characters, informationally spanned.

The revision (2026-07-14): the 1.10 “ceiling” itself was not an information limit. The deg-1+2 baseline has TRAIN KL 0.79 vs TEST 1.10 at M8000 — a 0.32-nat generalization gap — and test KL falls ≈ 0.175 nats per doubling of fibers (1.453 @ M2000 → 1.103 @ M8000). The val gate was rejecting degree-3 because it overfits at this M, mislabeling “overfit at small data” as “spanned”. The lever is data, not degree: a fixed FineWeb test set, a disjoint fresh training stream, the same GL basis, an ungated L2-regularized fit, and scale M until TEST ≤ 1.0.

One more boundary worth remembering: the encoding geometry matters only in bottlenecked students. Give a transformer the same masks through an embedding table and LSH vs random codes is a wash (21.2% vs 20.9% top-1) — the 0.18-nat effect lives entirely in models forced to read the code bits directly. That is the regime this whole page is about.

10The degree distribution of the top-1 — measured without fitting anything

Everything above finds structure by building it: enumerate characters, certify them, fit a student. This section measures where the variance of the top-1 target — the arc's agreement metric, \( f(x) \) = one-hot of the teacher's argmax over the full 248k-token vocabulary — lives across degrees directly. No basis, no code table, no fit. The probe: fork the teacher's cache at the token b positions back, resample that one token g = 16 times from the teacher's own conditional (real suffix teacher-forced back in), and check whether the argmax moved. For a one-hot function the within-fiber variance is a probability: Sensb = P(resampling the token b back flips the top-1), and Var = P(two random contexts disagree on the top-1) = 0.985. Parseval turns coordinates into degrees — a degree-k character is counted by exactly k coordinates:

$$ \mathrm{Sens}_b \;=\; \sum_{S\,:\,S\text{ touches }b}\lVert\hat f(S)\rVert^2, \qquad \bar S \;=\; \sum_b \mathrm{Sens}_b \;=\; \sum_{k\ge1} k\,\overline W^{\,k}, \qquad d_{\mathrm{eff}} \;=\; \frac{\bar S}{\mathrm{Var}} \;=\; \frac{\sum_k k\,\overline W^{\,k}}{\sum_k \overline W^{\,k}} , $$

so \( d_{\mathrm{eff}} \) is the variance-weighted average degree of the top-1 function — the center of mass of its degree distribution — obtained purely from single-token perturbations.

The measured flip-probability profile (M=1000 real FineWeb spans, ±1 s.e.). Two regimes: fast decay near the prediction (60% at the last token, 18% three back), then a plateau at 2–3% per position from b≈23 all the way out to b=124, with an uptick at the earliest tokens (the ones that set the topic). The tail is signal, not noise — identical resampled tokens produce identical rows, so flips only come from real substitutions.
What a mean degree of ≈5.4 pins down: the assumption-free Markov envelope — variance above degree K is at most \( (d_{\mathrm{eff}}-1)/(K-1) \) — decays slowly, so the mass is genuinely spread across degrees. Contrast the old distributional target (§9's KL arc), whose mean degree was ≈1: the top-1 function is not low-degree. Argmax flips are decided at near-ties resolved by long-range context.

The degree bound, live

The learning theorem needs tail mass \( \le \varepsilon/4 \) above the cutoff degree; Markov delivers it at \( d \ge 4\bar S/\varepsilon \). Drag the target error and watch the required cutoff move as 1/ε — for the top-1 target every practical ε demands degrees far beyond any windowed parity student. This is the fit-free explanation of why held-out top-1 agreement barely moved for every window student in the arc: the lever is context coverage, not window degree.
