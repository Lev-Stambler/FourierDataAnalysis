# Related-Work Survey — *Characterizing Datasets: Hardness, Learning, and More*

> **Status:** research brief for review. Nothing here is in the paper yet.
> **Method:** three independent literature sweeps (GL/sparse-Fourier; Fourier ML-auditing lineage; datasets/learning-theory/testing), each verified against arXiv / ECCC / publisher pages. Confidence and caveats are flagged inline; §10 lists everything to re-check before quoting.
>
> **What the paper does (for context):** Fourier analysis over a finite dataset `D ⊂ T^n` — dataset coefficients `f̂_D(S) = E_{x~D}[f(x)χ_S(x)]`, dataset sensitivity/influence, and **Goldreich–Levin over datasets**. Three technical pillars:
> 1. **`C_D` defect** — over `D` the parity characters `χ_S` are *not* orthonormal, so Parseval fails by `C_D = 2^n/|D|` (Mass Identity).
> 2. **Blindness** — samples-only access is provably insufficient (a birthday-horizon: once the dataset's projection onto un-split coordinates is collision-free, every bucket weight is identical, carrying no signal).
> 3. **Bias-spectrum convolution** — `f̂_D(S) = Σ_V b_V f̂(SΔV)` with `b_V = E_{x~D}[χ_V(x)]`; with `‖f̂‖₁ ≤ A` and knowledge of the heavy bias set, this resurrects Kushilevitz–Mansour over datasets from model queries.

## 1. Closest prior art & novelty assessment (read this first)

**The one-sentence positioning.** Every prior approach that leaves the uniform measure **re-orthonormalizes the basis to the distribution** (Gram–Schmidt), so a true Parseval identity survives and there is *no* `C_D`, *no* aliasing, and *no* blindness. This paper makes the opposite choice — **keep the fixed parity characters `χ_S`, change the measure to uniform-over-`D`** — and confronts non-orthogonality head-on. That single choice *is* the source of all three pillars. This is the contrast to lead with; it is also why "we move off the uniform measure" is **not** a valid novelty claim on its own (AFA and Heidari already do).

**Two highest-collision-risk works — engage directly:**

- **Heidari & Khardon 2025, "Learning DNF through Generalized Fourier Representations"** — arXiv:2506.01075. KM-style heavy-coefficient recovery under a *fixed non-uniform distribution*, using the *same* bounded-`‖f̂‖₁` hypothesis. **Differentiators to state explicitly:** (a) their basis is orthonormal (`E[φ_S]=0, E[φ_S²]=1`) so Parseval holds — no `C_D`; (b) the distribution is *given* as a Bayesian network, not accessed through an empirical sample; (c) they *require membership queries*; (d) no convolution identity, no samples-only impossibility. This is the single most likely "isn't this the same?" objection — pre-empt it. *(Comparison drawn from abstract/HTML; confirm points (c)–(d) against the final text — see §10.)*
- **Reing, Ver Steeg & Galstyan 2025, "Making Sense of Distributed Representations with Activation Spectroscopy"** — arXiv:2501.15435. Treats a hidden-layer→logit sub-network as a pseudo-Boolean function and finds high-value, **non-redundant** Fourier coefficients via what it explicitly calls **"an extension of the Goldreich–Levin algorithm with additional problem-specific constraints,"** operating over an **in-distribution** activation support (it argues uniform inputs are "practically useless"). It independently hits three of this paper's signature moves — in-distribution Boolean-Fourier, coefficient redundancy (≈ the aliasing / `C_D` issue), and GL-over-data. It is an *applied interpretability heuristic* (no `C_D` theory, no blindness, no learning/testing formalism), so it does not pre-empt the theory — but it must be cited and contrasted, likely in the GL section.

**Supporting anchors (each pillar has a known ancestor — frame as *sharpening*, not first discovery):**

- *Blindness* ← the samples-vs-queries barrier: **SQ / noisy-parity** (Kearns 1993/98; Blum–Kalai–Wasserman 2003) says correlational/sample access cannot isolate a heavy parity; **PAC-verification / interactive GL** (Goldwasser–Rothblum–Shafer–Yehudayoff, ITCS 2021; Gur et al., STOC 2024) is the "samples alone are too weak → restore power via a prover" thesis. Your remedy substitutes *bias-spectrum side information* for the prover.
- *Convolution* ← **aliasing under subsampling** in sparse Walsh–Hadamard transforms (SparseFHT 2015; SPRIGHT 2015): subsampling the time domain *is* a convolution/mixing of Fourier coefficients. The difference: they *design* invertible aliasing with full query access; your `D` is *given/adversarial*, so the aliasing is not yours to choose — exactly why blindness bites and you need the bias spectrum.
- *`Sens_i` / influence* ← the **Sobol total-effect index** of variance-based global sensitivity analysis (Sobol; Hooker): `Sens_i[f] = E[Var(f | x_{-i})]` is literally the total-effect Sobol index, and its Fourier form `Σ_{S∋i} f̂(S)²` is the classical spectral ANOVA. The subtlety your `#CLAUDE` notes catch (the identity is clean only for *product* measures) is the classical ANOVA/Hoeffding-decomposition non-product subtlety.
- *Data-adapted Fourier* ← **Heidari et al. 2021** (the expansion **AFA itself uses**) and **arbitrary Polynomial Chaos** (Oladyshkin–Nowak 2012), both of which re-orthonormalize to an arbitrary/empirical measure.

**Novelty verdict (skeptical).** No prior work does all three pillars over the *empirical/uniform-on-dataset* measure with the *fixed* character basis. The **synthesis appears genuinely novel**; the **components each have strong precedent**. Reviewer-proofing means: (1) distinguish from Heidari–Khardon explicitly; (2) cite Activation Spectroscopy; (3) present blindness as a dataset-specific quantitative refinement of the SQ / interactive-GL barrier; (4) present the convolution identity as the "given, not designed" cousin of subsampling-aliasing. Closest single prior art is a two-way tie: **Heidari–Khardon 2025** (for the "GL/KM under a fixed non-uniform distribution + bounded `‖f̂‖₁`" program) and **GRSY 2021 / Gur 2024** (for the samples-insufficiency thesis).

## 2. Classical Goldreich–Levin lineage (the primitive being generalized)

- **`goldreich1989hardcore`** — Goldreich & Levin, *A hard-core predicate for all one-way functions*, STOC 1989. The original list-decoding / heavy-coefficient algorithm; **query access**, uniform measure. → The primitive your dataset-GL generalizes; cite in the classical-GL recap (`gl.typ:51`).
- **`kushilevitz1993learning`** — Kushilevitz & Mansour, *Learning decision trees using the Fourier spectrum*, STOC 1991 / SICOMP 22(6):1331–1348, 1993. GL applied to learn sparse / low-`‖f̂‖₁` functions; **membership queries**, uniform. → Your direct baseline for "KM over datasets" (`gl.typ:296`).
- **`akavia2003proving`** — Akavia, Goldwasser & Safra, *Proving hard-core predicates using list decoding*, FOCS 2003. Extends GL/KM to find significant coefficients over **any finite abelian group** (the SFT algorithm, notably `Z_N`); query access, Haar/uniform on the group. → Precedent for your "general finite abelian group" remark, but still the group's uniform measure, not an empirical dataset.
- **`akavia2018finding`** — Akavia, *Finding Significant Fourier Coefficients: Clarifications, Simplifications, Applications and Limitations*, Chicago J. Theoretical CS 2018 (arXiv:1607.01842); also *Deterministic sparse Fourier approximation via fooling arithmetic progressions*, COLT 2010. SFT over finite abelian groups incl. a "Limitations" section. → **Check the Limitations section** — it may or may not resemble blindness (PDF was unreadable to the sweep; §10).
- **`mansour1994learning`** — Mansour, *Learning Boolean functions via the Fourier transform*, survey, 1994. Standard framing of KM / low-degree / sparse learning under uniform. → Positioning reference.
- **`dewolf2008brief`** — de Wolf, *A Brief Introduction to Fourier Analysis on the Boolean Cube*, Theory of Computing Library, 2008. → Baseline that the empirical coefficient `(1/m)Σ f·χ_S` converges to `f̂(S)` — the starting point of your sample-access analysis.

## 3. Non-uniform / data-adapted Fourier & heavy-coefficient learning (the core comparison class)

- **`odonnell2014analysis` / `o2021analysis`** (already in bib) — O'Donnell, *Analysis of Boolean Functions*, 2014, **Ch. 8 (p-biased)**. `φ_S(x)=∏_{i∈S}(x_i−μ)/σ`. → Load-bearing contrast: a *product* measure still yields an *orthonormal* basis, so **Parseval holds** — precisely what fails for a general dataset. Use to motivate `C_D`: p-biased is the largest well-behaved non-uniform family; the dataset measure is the step beyond product measures where orthonormality breaks.
- **`heidari2021finding`** — Heidari, Sreedharan, Shamir & Szpankowski, *Finding Relevant Information via a Discrete Fourier Expansion*, ICML 2021, PMLR 139:4181–4191. Generalizes Boolean Fourier **beyond product measures** to correlated variables via a distribution-orthonormalized basis; used for feature selection. → **The expansion AFA adopts.** The closest existing formalization of "Fourier coefficients over a data distribution"; cite alongside your construction and note the basis-vs-measure difference.
- **`heidari2025learning`** — Heidari & Khardon, *Learning DNF through Generalized Fourier Representations*, 2025, arXiv:2506.01075. (See §1 — highest collision risk.)
- **`oladyshkin2012data`** — Oladyshkin & Nowak, *Data-driven uncertainty quantification using the arbitrary polynomial chaos expansion (aPC)*, Reliability Engineering & System Safety 106:179–190, 2012. Builds an orthonormal polynomial basis w.r.t. an **arbitrary measure specified only as a histogram / raw dataset** (finitely many moments, no density). → The UQ-community analogue of "orthonormal basis over a general measure, instantiated on the empirical distribution." Sharp contrast: aPC *re-orthogonalizes* (no aliasing, no `C_D`); you keep fixed characters and confront it.
- **`furst1991improved`** — Furst, Jackson & Smith, *Improved learning of AC⁰ functions*, COLT 1991. Fourier learning of AC⁰ under **product** distributions. → Classic non-uniform case, but product ⇒ orthonormal ⇒ no `C_D`.
- **`jackson1997efficient`** — Jackson, *An efficient membership-query algorithm for learning DNF w.r.t. the uniform distribution (the Harmonic Sieve)*, FOCS 1994 / JCSS 55(3):414–440, 1997. Boosting reweights the measure, so KM must find heavy parities under a *sequence of non-uniform (reweighted) distributions*. → The classic "heavy-Fourier-finding under non-uniform measures," but distributions derived from uniform, with MQ — not a fixed empirical dataset.
- **`gopalan2008agnostically`** — Gopalan, Kalai & Klivans, *Agnostically learning decision trees*, STOC 2008. L1-regression over the `2^n`-dim L1 ball with KM as heavy-coefficient oracle; uniform, MQ. → Your `‖f̂‖₁ ≤ A` hypothesis is exactly the L1 geometry these methods exploit; cite at the KM-over-datasets step.
- **`feldman2007attribute`** — Feldman, *Attribute-efficient and non-adaptive learning of parities and DNF expressions*, COLT 2005 / JMLR 8:1431–1460, 2007; and **`kalai2008agnostic`** — Kalai, Mansour & Verbin, *On agnostic boosting and parity learning*, STOC 2008. Under uniform, agnostic-parity ≡ noisy-parity; `2^{O(n/log n)}` algorithms. → Context for how hard heavy-parity-finding becomes off the clean uniform-query setting.
- **`bshouty2004more`** — Bshouty, Jackson & Tamon, *More efficient PAC-learning of DNF with membership queries under the uniform distribution*, COLT 1999 / JCSS 68(2):205–234, 2004 (ECCC TR98-076). Notable: develops techniques for **lower bounding the sample size for PAC learning with MQ under a fixed distribution**. → A mild ancestor of your quantitative blindness bound (sample lower bounds under a fixed measure); different technique.

## 4. Samples-vs-queries barrier (the conceptual home of "blindness")

- **`kearns1998sq`** — Kearns, *Efficient noise-tolerant learning from statistical queries*, STOC 1993 / JACM 45(6):983–1006, 1998. The SQ model. → Root of "sample/correlational access is strictly weaker than queries."
- **`blum1994weakly`** — Blum, Furst, Jackson, Kearns, Mansour & Rudich, *Weakly learning DNF and characterizing SQ learning using Fourier analysis*, STOC 1994. SQ-dimension via Fourier; parities are pairwise near-orthogonal ⇒ SQ cannot isolate a heavy parity. → The Fourier-analytic form of the barrier; closest in spirit to your bucket-blindness.
- **`blum2003noise`** — Blum, Kalai & Wasserman, *Noise-tolerant learning, the parity problem, and the SQ model*, JACM 50(4):506–519, 2003. → Canonical hardness-of-parity-from-samples reference.
- **`goldwasser2021interactive`** — Goldwasser, Rothblum, Shafer & Yehudayoff, *Interactive proofs for verifying machine learning*, ITCS 2021 (ECCC TR20-058). PAC-verification: for Fourier-sparse functions the prover uses MQ while the verifier uses only random samples. → Closest predecessor to your sample-insufficiency thesis — a sample-only party can't recover heavy coefficients, so it borrows query power. Your remedy: bias-spectrum side info instead of a prover.
- **`gur2024power`** — Gur, Jahanara, Khodabandeh, Rajgopal, Salamatian & Shinkar, *On the Power of Interactive Proofs for Learning*, STOC 2024, arXiv:2404.08158. An **"interactive Goldreich–Levin"**: a verifier with only random examples recovers the `t` largest coefficients with a prover's help. → The most explicit "GL from random samples only (+ prover)" in the literature; direct successor to GRSY.
- **`kocaoglu2014sparse`** — Kocaoglu, Shanmugam, Dimakis & Klivans, *Sparse polynomial learning and graph sketching*, NIPS 2014, arXiv:1402.3902. Recovers an `s`-sparse polynomial from **random uniform samples only** under a "unique sign property." → Rare positive "samples-only sparse Fourier" result — a useful contrast: it needs uniform sampling + a strong structural crutch, exactly what the bias spectrum replaces in your setting.

## 5. Aliasing / sparse Walsh–Hadamard / sparse set functions (the convolution mechanism)

- **`scheibler2015fast`** — Scheibler, Haghighatshoar & Vetterli, *A Fast Hadamard Transform for Signals with Sub-linear Sparsity in the Transform Domain (SparseFHT)*, IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 61(4):2115–2132, 2015 (arXiv:1310.1803). Subsampling the time domain deliberately **aliases** WHT coefficients; a sparse-graph peeling decoder unmixes them with `O(K log(N/K))` samples. → **Mechanistic twin of your convolution identity.** Difference: they *design* invertible aliasing with query access; your dataset's aliasing (the bias spectrum) is given/adversarial.
- **`li2015spright`** — Li, Bradley, Pawar & Ramchandran, *SPRIGHT: A robust and noise-tolerant sparse Walsh–Hadamard transform*, 2015, arXiv:1508.06336. Robust version of the above. → Cite alongside SparseFHT as the compressed-sensing lineage of the convolution identity.
- **`stobbe2012learning`** — Stobbe & Krause, *Learning Fourier Sparse Set Functions*, AISTATS 2012. `k`-sparse set functions from `O(k·polylog n)` random set evaluations via compressed sensing / RIP. → Ancestor of sparse-Hadamard learning; contrast: chosen sampling with RIP, whereas your dataset's measure is not yours to choose (RIP-style incoherence fails ⇒ `C_D`, blindness).
- **`amrollahi2019efficiently`** — Amrollahi, Zandieh, Kapralov & Krause, *Efficiently Learning Fourier Sparse Set Functions*, NeurIPS 2019. `k`-sparse, low-degree-supported set functions from `O(kd log n)` evaluations via spectrum hashing/subsampling. → Modern sparse-Hadamard learning; the set-function analogue of dataset-GL (query access, uniform, no non-orthogonality).
- **`wendler2021learning`** — Wendler, Amrollahi, Seifert, Krause & Püschel, *Learning Set Functions that are Sparse in Non-Orthogonal Fourier Bases*, AAAI 2021, arXiv:2010.00439. Recovery for set functions sparse in **non-orthogonal** (set-lattice / DSFT) Fourier bases. → The prior work that most directly embraces *non-orthonormal* Fourier characters — but the non-orthogonality is algebraic (set-containment), not from an empirical measure; query access, no aliasing/blindness.
- **`erginbas2023efficiently`** — Erginbas, Kang, Aghazadeh & Ramchandran, *Efficiently Computing Sparse Fourier Transforms of q-ary Functions*, ISIT 2023, arXiv:2301.06200. The sparse-WHT / q-ary-Fourier backbone behind SPEX/SHAP-zero. → Modern query-efficient sparse-Fourier machinery a dataset-GL paper competes with / can cite as an alternative to classical GL; also relevant to your `T = tokens/q-ary` generalization.

## 6. Fourier auditing / interpretability of trained models (where arXiv:2410.08111 lives)

### The target paper, read closely
- **`ajarra2024active`** — Ajarra, Ghosh & Basu, *Active Fourier Auditor for Estimating Distributional Properties of ML Models*, arXiv:2410.08111, **AAAI 2025**. **Access model:** black-box **label queries** to a classifier `h` **plus samples from the data distribution `𝒟`** used to generate query points. **Fourier definition:** a **`𝒟`-orthonormalized** (Gram–Schmidt) basis `{ψ_S}` with `ĥ(S)=E_{x~𝒟}[h ψ_S]` — imported from Heidari 2021, **not** the uniform-measure Walsh basis. **Properties as functionals:** robustness `=Σ_S ρ^{|S|}ĥ(S)²`, individual fairness `=Σ_S ρ^{|S_l|}ĥ(S)²`, group fairness via a quadratic in `ĥ(∅)` and `Σ_{S∋A}ĥ(S)²`. **Algorithm:** a **GL-style significant-coefficient finder** adapted to `𝒟` (bucket tree, split/prune), then plug `L_h` into the functional. **Rates:** `Õ(1/ε)` samples (robustness / individual fairness), `Õ(1/ε²)` (group fairness), matching `Ω̃(δ/ε²)` lower bound. → **Cleanest contrast for your paper:** *basis vs. measure* — AFA orthonormalizes the basis to `𝒟` (Parseval survives, loses the parity interpretation); you keep parities and change the measure (parity interpretation survives, Parseval breaks → `C_D`). Also: AFA estimates 2–3 scalar functionals from a sampleable population; you recover the *spectrum* of `f` over a *fixed finite* `D`. AFA instantiated on the empirical measure ≈ an orthonormalized GL-over-dataset; your differentiators are the fixed basis, non-orthogonality, blindness, and coefficient recovery. Resolves the `gl.typ:319` TODO. *(Theorem constants read from HTML — verify against the AAAI PDF; §10.)*

### Ghosh–Basu fairness-verification lineage (supplies the *fairness semantics*, not the Fourier machinery — none of the first three use Fourier)
- **`ghosh2021justicia`** — Ghosh, Basu & Meel, *Justicia: A Stochastic SAT Approach to Formally Verify Fairness*, AAAI 2021, arXiv:2009.06516.
- **`ghosh2022fvgm`** — Ghosh, Basu & Meel, *Algorithmic Fairness Verification with Graphical Models*, AAAI 2022 (oral), arXiv:2109.09447. Introduces "fairness influence functions."
- **`ghosh2023fairness`** — Ghosh, Basu & Meel, *"How Biased are Your Features?": Computing Fairness Influence Functions with Global Sensitivity Analysis*, FAccT 2023, arXiv:2206.00667. Variance-based (Sobol) decomposition of a fairness metric — the continuous cousin of AFA's Fourier level-weights. → Bridges §6 to §7 (Sobol).

- **`yan2022active`** — Yan & Zhang, *Active Fairness Auditing*, ICML 2022, arXiv:2206.08450. **AFA's direct predecessor / baseline:** query-based estimation of demographic parity via version-space / agnostic-active-learning. → Contrasting AFA (Fourier concentration) against Yan–Zhang (version spaces) is the sharpest "why Fourier" motivation; relevant to your access-model discussion.
- **`bafa2026audit`** — *Audit Me If You Can: Query-Efficient Active Fairness Auditing of Black-Box LLMs*, Findings of ACL 2026, arXiv:2601.03087. Approximates a trained model by a **sparse Walsh–Hadamard expansion**, then reads SHAP in closed form. → Same "recover the model's Boolean-Fourier spectrum, then answer downstream property queries" pattern as your auditing framing.

## 7. Sensitivity / influence / feature importance (the `Sens_i` machinery)

- **`sobol2001global`** — Sobol, *Global sensitivity indices for nonlinear mathematical models and their Monte Carlo estimates*, Math. Comput. Simul. 55:271–280, 2001 (orig. 1993). The **total-effect index** `E[Var(f|x_{-i})] = E[(f−E^i f)²]`. → **`Sens_i[f]` is exactly the Sobol total-effect index**, and `Σ_{S∋i}f̂(S)²` is its spectral (ANOVA) form. Most important "you are generalizing X" connection for the sensitivity sections; also the source of the product-measure caveat behind your `#CLAUDE` corrections.
- **`hooker2007generalized`** — Hooker, *Generalized Functional ANOVA Diagnostics for High-Dimensional Functions of Dependent Variables*, J. Comput. Graph. Stat. 16(3):709–732, 2007. Functional ANOVA under **dependent** (non-product) inputs. → The precise reference for why the Sobol=Fourier identity needs care over the non-product empirical measure `D`.
- **`datta2016algorithmic`** — Datta, Sen & Zick, *Algorithmic Transparency via Quantitative Input Influence (QII)*, IEEE S&P 2016. Axiomatic Banzhaf/Shapley-style feature influence, estimated by **perturbing features and averaging over a dataset**. → Direct precedent for computing influence *from an empirical dataset* — the applied ML instantiation of dataset sensitivity.
- **`novak2018sensitivity`** — Novak, Bahri, Abolafia, Pennington & Sohl-Dickstein, *Sensitivity and Generalization in Neural Networks*, ICLR 2018, arXiv:1802.08760. Input–output Jacobian sensitivity **on/near the data manifold** predicts generalization. → "Sensitivity computed over the dataset, not uniform" — the empirical, continuous cousin; bridge to §8.
- *(Secondary)* Shapley/Banzhaf–Fourier closed forms — e.g. *Expected Shapley-like scores of Boolean functions* (PODS/PACMMOD 2024) and FourierSHAP-style work (2025). → Confirms attributions have clean Fourier/Möbius forms; positions dataset influence within game-theoretic attribution.

## 8. Distribution-specific learnability & dataset hardness (the "hardness" of the title; resolves `idea.typ:230`)

- **`benedek1991learnability`** — Benedek & Itai, *Learnability with respect to fixed distributions*, Theoretical Computer Science 86(2):377–389, 1991. A class is learnable under a **single fixed measure `μ` iff its ε-covering number (metric entropy) under `L¹(μ)` is finite**. → **The canonical answer to your TODO.** "Hardness of a dataset" = metric entropy of the class under the dataset's own empirical measure. Backbone of the distribution-specific-learnability framing.
- **`kearns1987recent`** — Kearns, Li, Pitt & Valiant, *Recent results on Boolean concept learning*, Proc. 4th Int. Workshop on Machine Learning, 1987. Credited as first to introduce **distribution-specific** learning. → Historical origin of "learning relative to a fixed/structured distribution."
- **`vapnik1998statistical`** — Vapnik, *Statistical Learning Theory*, 1998. VC dimension ⇒ distribution-free uniform convergence; distribution-*dependent* refinements beat universal bounds for fixed distributions. → The distribution-free baseline you depart from; the "distribution-agnostic learning theory" your TODO names.
- **`canatar2021spectral`** — Canatar, Bordelon & Pehlevan, *Spectral bias and task–model alignment explain generalization in kernel regression and infinitely wide neural networks*, Nature Communications 12:2914, 2021, arXiv:2006.13198. Decomposes the target over the **data-dependent kernel eigenbasis**; learnability is governed by how the target's spectral weight aligns with top data eigenmodes. → **Closest formal answer** to your "degree over a dataset vs. full space ⇒ learnability?" question, in the continuous/kernel setting; your "degree over `D`" is a Boolean analogue.
- **`abbe2023sgd`** — Abbe, Boix-Adserà & Misiakiewicz, *SGD learning on neural networks: leap complexity and saddle-to-saddle dynamics*, COLT 2023, arXiv:2302.11055; and **`abbe2021staircase`** — *The staircase property*, NeurIPS 2021, arXiv:2108.10573. **Hierarchical Fourier complexity** (staircase / leap) of a target predicts SGD sample/time complexity. → State-of-the-art "intrinsic Fourier hardness of a target ⇒ learning cost" — your thesis, for uniform/Gaussian inputs. Best current benchmark for "spectral hardness ⇒ learning."
- **`ethayarajh2022understanding`** — Ethayarajh, Choi & Swayamdipta, *Understanding Dataset Difficulty with V-Usable Information*, ICML 2022 (Outstanding Paper), arXiv:2110.08420. **Dataset difficulty relative to a model family `𝒱`** via `𝒱`-usable information. → The information-theoretic counterpart of your goal ("hardness of a dataset relative to a model class"); natural comparison, different toolset.
- **`mixon2015learning`** — Mixon & Peterson, *Learning Boolean functions with concentrated spectra*, 2015, arXiv:1507.04319. Bounds VC dimension of Fourier-concentrated classes; efficient ERM; **applied to MNIST**. → An actual instance of Boolean-Fourier analysis on a real dataset linking spectral concentration to sample complexity.
- *(Secondary)* **`valleperez2019deep`** — Valle-Pérez, Camargo & Louis, *Deep learning generalizes because the parameter–function map is biased towards simple functions*, ICLR 2019, arXiv:1805.08522. Simplicity bias over Boolean functions. → Adjacent complexity-of-target story supporting the low-degree/low-sensitivity emphasis.
- *(Applied, secondary)* Spectral dataset-complexity heuristics: Branchaud-Charron et al., *Spectral Metric for Dataset Complexity Assessment*, CVPR 2019; *Demystifying Spectral Bias on Real-World Data*, arXiv:2406.02663, 2024.

## 9. Property testing over datasets / support (the "in-distribution testing" notion)

- **`halevy2007distribution`** — Halevy & Kushilevitz, *Distribution-free property testing*, RANDOM 2003 / SIAM J. Comput. 37(4):1107–1138, 2007. Distance measured w.r.t. a **fixed but unknown distribution**, with sample + query access; reduction from uniform testers + self-correction. → Canonical model for "testing a property over the dataset's distribution." Difference: your in-distribution testing additionally zeroes out-of-dataset points (restricts to the support).
- **`chen2018distribution`** — Chen, Liu, Servedio, et al., *Distribution-free junta testing*, arXiv:1802.04859, 2018 (+ follow-ups). Adaptive distribution-free `k`-junta testing in `Õ(k²)/ε` queries; exponential adaptive-vs-nonadaptive separation. → Parallels a "does `f` depend on few coordinates over `D`" primitive; connects to your dataset sensitivity/influence.
- **`chen2024relative`** — Relative-error property testing: Chen, De & Huang, *Relative-error monotonicity testing*, arXiv:2410.09235, 2024; Chen, Pires, Pitassi & Servedio (conjunctions/decision lists), ICALP 2025; *Testing Juntas Optimally with Relative Error*, arXiv:2504.09312, 2025. Distance measured **relative to `|f⁻¹(1)|`**, tester gets **uniform samples from the support** — motivated because sparse functions are trivially close to constant-0 under standard distance. → **Very close motivation** to your in-distribution testing (distance over `D`, not uniform Hamming); strongly worth engaging.
